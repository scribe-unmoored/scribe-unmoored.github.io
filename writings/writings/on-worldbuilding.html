On Worldbuilding

By Kark


In this review, I will be examining a long-awaited entry in my series. A book famous for being the longest in the known universe—so long in fact, that it has never been completed. I am speaking, of course, of Gravidus the Egg-Shaped’s seminal work, “On the Construction of Planes, Spheres, Minds, and Other Habitable Objects.”

I encountered it on a long-anticipated visit to the Grand Libraterra of Epsilon Eridani III, where it spans the greater part of the subcontinent. A great system of scaffolds has been constructed over the continental shelf there, permitting the book to be opened fully. In a previous epoch, when book had remained closed, it served as the ecological basis for a host of new life, rising from the sheer geological scale of its permanence. Pageworms, ink-bearing flowers, and even a species of bipedal phorusrachian have evolved to call the Lexical Plain home. When it was first opened, the back cover plunged the eastern sea into darkness, creating what is now known as the abyssal shelf, wherein all manner of creatures have begun to migrate in an ecological succession of unprecedented proportions. In the same manner that life came to occupy the gargantuan pages of Construction, new life, rising from the darkened depths, has been catalogued beneath the back cover, flourishing in a world of eternal darkness. It is believed that the original author must have had it transported to the planet by some esoteric means, only to realize that the book could not be opened without losing a sizable sheaf to the ocean. Whatever became of Gravidus after this event, none can say.

The script of those immense pages is minuscule—no larger than the words I write here. Even the most erudite of readers are unlikely to complete a single page in their lifetime. Many scholars have dedicated as much time to digesting only a single paragraph. In my few days there1, stalking the great catwalks between supply repositories, I was able to take notes on a sizable portion of the first clause. After some time playing the role of scholar, I returned from the pagelands, where I studied the numerous exhibits available for tourists curious about the book’s contents.

There I learned the history of the book’s mapping, a long and arduous process beginning in the early twenty-eighth century. The acquisition of core samples was one of the first major breakthroughs in mapping the titanic book, though this is done with excessive rarity and with care to document every word extracted and every page punctured by the extraction drill. Even with this new development, the vast majority of the book remained elusive to scholars. In fact, librarians failed for decades to extract anything beyond the first chapter, even after drilling an estimated two-fifths of the thickness of the second half of the book. That was, until the development of extremely precise hypersonic imaging techniques, so named “the spinal tap” because it utilizes the structure of—what has been dubbed—the Great Chartaceous Uplift Plain2 to seismographically map the book’s contents.

The initial mapping revealed that the first chapter comprises the majority of the book. Seventy percent of Construction is dedicated to this chapter alone, which—as far as estimates can demonstrate—details the intricate process of constructing a functioning system of metaphysics ex nihilo.

The first clause, which I attempted to study myself, explains that the construction of any system, coherent or otherwise, begins with a few fundamental axioms. These axioms—or at least, the ones I was able to read in my time with the book, are as follows:

1. An initial decision must be made as to whether the constructed universe will operate via a causal or noncausal happenstance. That is, “Does cause and effect apply in this universe?” Several considerations are provided for noncausal realities: First, they must operate almost entirely on coincidence—everything occurs nonlinearly in such a universe, and therefore becomes perfectly predictable in that all things occur with equal probability. Gravidus points out that this is an obscenely amateur technique, often utilized by impatient worldbuilders interested in only a single event, such as biogenesis. The author recommends utilizing such a universe—of infinite size—to act as a reference for any potential contingencies which might arise spontaneously in normal causality.

2. The general shape of a universe is something Gravidus makes a brief footnote of. He mentions that no matter the number of dimensions, the hyperresolution of a created universe remains unbounded in scale. That is, an eleventh dimension will appear incomprehensibly shapeless to a being standing in the twenty-seventh dimension, and so on in the same way that it is difficult for a being of the third dimension to imagine a monodimensional space. Gravidus helpfully supplies a formula in a footnote which describes the comprehensibility overlap of adjacent dimensions. (I did not visit the footnote myself. A handily erected signpost gave a brief summary of the information, which was located over a thousand kilometers away.) Apparently, dimensional comprehensibility is geometric in nature, growing with access to each subsequent higher dimension, but not necessarily encompassing all lower dimensions. For example, we can articulate a first, second, third, and barely a fourth dimension as three-dimensional beings. A ninth-dimensional being can theoretically articulate up to twelve dimensions, a fifteenth-dimensional being can articulate twenty, and so on. The Gravidian Optimal, which the author claims to be the perfect number of dimensions for a causal universe, is forty-two.

3. The last axiom I was able to record was perhaps the most complicated, detailing the intricacies inherent in deciding whether or not the universe should be ontological, phenomenological, or symbolic in nature. A symbolic universe (perhaps analogous to the primitive software simulations of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries) is one of the easiest to produce, according to Gravidus, as it can be built with a presumptive framework based on the creator’s own experiences. The author claims that this is the best sort of universe for beginners, as it introduces them to the fundamentals without the burden of designing every detail themselves. Of the other two universes, Gravidus states that he prefers the phenomenological, enjoying the benefits and low cost of self-situated monism.

An additional seventy-three axioms are listed in the first clause, while I learned from an exhibit that the second clause separates them into categories based on ease of integration. Thus ends the first sentence of the first page, about two kilometers east of where my visit to the great book ended. The remainder of the page, though not fully documented, details every method by which these axioms can be monadically situated.

Chapter two is much less well-studied, but is thought to exhaustively detail numerous processes of biogenesis, a number of which are predicated on the contents of chapter three, which is only known by its title: “III: Heaven, Deities, Pantheons, and the Consequences of Angering God.”
