Enumerations of the Antecessors

By the Scribe Unmoored

Translation by Jorel Flauvius, Scholar of Meric Philology


A song begins with the first notes, and ends with the last. All that breathes and dies follows this, for that is all existence is—a song.

Before the song begins, Time is empty; because there is not yet a beginning to teach it. There was only Anu, the Thinking god. The Dreamer. The one with two faces: I AM, and I AM NOT

Both faces, though they were brothers, hated one another. I AM clove NOT from IS, and this rive is called CHIM—the Understanding. All that is emanates from the binary crux: the interplay of the brothers and the strife between them. First, Anu struck his brother Padomay, who was once I AM NOT—the Face that Cannot See. One blow was met with another, and met with another, and met with another.

I AM.

I AM NOT.

I AM.

I AM NOT.

I AM.

I AM NOT.

The drum of their violence stirred heaven. Akatosh, who is Time, Father of Dragons, was counted into being by the rain of their blows. The first moment was his. The elves worship him as Auri-El—though all save the Epopt say his name wrong now. Man worships him, as do all that know themselves apart from the trees and stones, but their name for Aiae-A’el is even more wrong (this spelling is the closest that exists in Tamrielic, and it is pronounced as the sound a child makes when they first cry for the teat). Akatosh begat Alduin, the End. That is all I will say on the matter of the World-Eater, the Silence-at-the-End-of-All-Song. To speak his name bears ill-will and malice.

In the beginning, light and darkness were the first weapons Anu and Padomay wielded against one another. When Anu first spilt the blood of his brother, the wound was wide and deep, bleeding nought. From it, slithered Sithis. We know the Dread Father’s name because it was carved into the first tongue that spoke it.

I AM NOT saw that Aiae-A’el could count, and so created an un-heart to be filled with Time’s beating. Then was born Padomay’s second son, La-[NULL], whom the elves call Lorkhan. Men have as many names for Lorkhan as Lorkhan has bastards. The Khajiit call him Lorkaj. Contrary to popular belief, the Khajiiti translation precedes the elven one.

(Footnote: The direct LHNOian translation of Lorkhan’s name cannot be rendered in full. Here, it is rendered as La-[NULL], for the safety of the reader.)

Anu and Padomay, resigned to their roles as brothers and enemies, called for a truce. From it, the Et’Ada were born—ruminations and resentment borne from their brittle peace. It is said that this happened instantaneously, as Akatosh was looking away when it happened.


Here follows the naming of the ancestor spirits, the eight brothers and sisters of Akatosh:

Ma’Rah, whose name is love. The sisters of Ma’Rah do not have names—but their Names are Grief, and Despair, and Loneliness, and Fellowship.

Kchi, who is the wind and breath, which is the wind in all things that breathe.

DBLA. This name cannot be spelled fully because it will cause the heavens to blush and the ground to wet. For the safety—and dignity—of the reader, the goddess of love and beauty is rendered in this shortened form.

Ar’Ka, whose name is life and death.

Yf, whose name is the answer to questions—who does not tolerate sameness among the different.

Ju-A’el, who first enumerated the number of Lorkhan’s heartbeats. His name is cried like a child who is angry. This is believed to be a jest, and perhaps the oldest.

Ste’ar, whose name is the balance of the heavens.

Sen, whose name is mastery.

(Footnote: In this translation of the Scribe’s work, translations have been taken from [EHLNOFEY ROOT TONGUE] and Ehlnofex-Fey. Any etymological and philological discrepancies are due to partial translations or interpretations based on historical context. The surviving works of the Scribe are few—so liberties have been taken in rendering these names.)

(Footnote: Et’Ada are gendered according to tradition. The Scribe’s mother tongue, widely believed to be [ORATOR-SCRIBE: THE LAW, THE LAST, AND THE DEAD], contains no gendered pronouns. This has had immense implications on the study of LHNO non-material culture.)

The elves call these the Aedra, which they say means “our ancestors,” or “ancestors.” This is a common misconception—so common that I will not stem a wound which has been bandaged a thousand times over. To quote the minotarch: to string a bow so close to dawn is like eating grass that has already died.

(Footnote: No one knows what this passage means.)

Lorkhan’s siblings have no names, for they exist outside of the holes their names leave. A hole is not a thing. This is widely understood.

(Footnote: And again.)


Lorkhan called to himself these names, and spoke every other word so that the words unspoken were the names of his siblings. Thus gathered all of the spirits, the ancestors and their enantiodromes. He said:

“[Anu] has named peace, and his prelude has quieted into one tone, which is peace. Now there is no silence for our mother Padomay, and our song will not end but endure forever into sameness. Build with me a new song, and there will be tears and screams and laughter and [the sounds of] lovemaking to fill it.”

Yf, who is not fain to sameness, was first to come forward. Akatosh was second, as is his prerogative. Together they built the world with Lorkhan, and so creation was born from a singular, great act:

Deception.

The ancestor spirits lifted their hands from their work, desiring rest, and found that they were manacled to the world of their making by chains of love, breath, and earth. This is when they cried out, some in propitiation, others in terror. No few cried out in joy. The Others, spirits whom watched from nearby, saw their chains and heard their cries and they fled.

Lorkhan, smiling as he died, saw that the bodies of his clan congealed into the world, and he died speaking its name, [Mundus], which bears Nir, the earthen-star.

Here follows the naming of the fleeing-spirits, whose wake is the sun and stars.

Mah, whose name is magic. Elves and men call him Magnus. He was first to flee, and so his wake is greatest. It rises in the east and sets in the west—still fleeing the darkness that follows it.

Ia, whose name is laughter when one should be distraught. Ra-A’el, everyone knows this name. It is said like the bawl of a dog when it is hungry. Namyr-Ra, whose name is when things are dying and loved. Ee, whose name is laughter and joy in the company of friends. Hh, whose name is laughter when there is a jest at one’s own expense and they are humble and of good temperament. Kh, whose name is “to eat dirt happily.” [Meridia], her name is burning light.

(Footnote: The original manuscript bearing the names of Magnus and the Magna-Ge is several thousand pages long and contains ten times as many entries in its listing of “the fleeing spirits.” Among these, numerous stars and constellations can be recognized even today. It is believed that the Magna-Ge made up the bulk of the LHNO pantheon, and that astrological navigation, star-worship, and the night sky were integral components of LHNO culture. A copy of the full text is available through request at the Palace Library.)


The Others that did not flee, but waited for the corpse of the Deceiver to cool, were called the Daedra by elves. We have no name for them, except that which they have asked to be called.

Here follows the naming of the demons, with whom no person should deal:

Azura. Her name is love, but her face is menace. It is imperative that she does not have Her Way. Boethia. Her name is lies, but she lies even of this. Her true name is poison. Malacath. His name is oaths. You may make an oath with him, and he will keep it. Do not do this. Mehrunes Dagon. His name is change, in forms violent and subtle. This name will not be written. You know of whom I speak: the Father of Vampires. Vaermina. Her name is the dream that kills. Hermaeus Mora. His name is secrets. Ithelia. Her name is forgotten. We alone keep it. Hircine. His name is fairness; in the way a cat is fair to the mouse. Mephala. Her name is an empty womb which draws seed and turns it to poison. Her name is lies, and was stolen by her brother-sister, Boethia. Clavicus Vile. His name is thievery, hidden beneath mercantilism. Jyggalag. [His name is perfect order.]

(Footnote: Jyggalag here is represented by two alternating symbols in the original text, followed by a mathematical proof of several well-known theorems— and one which was previously unknown to scholars.)

Nocturnal, whose name is night—the mother-adversary. She tries to hide the stars, stealing them away with night-ink, and so the night sky is black. Peryite, the false dragon—whose name is remembering and forgetting. Sanguine, whose name is excess. Our vomit be praise to him.

(Footnote: The LHNO word for Sanguine is uncannily similar to the heaving sound one makes before losing their lunch.)

Sheogorath, whose name is evil laughter and forgetting of necessary woes.


These are the remunerations of the antecessors. I, [The Scribe Unmoored], have listed them here, so that their names may be venerated, reviled, and never forgotten.

(Footnote: Despite references to merish, human, and Khajiiti cultures, LHNO language and society are believed to have died out sometime in the early Merethic Era, pre-dating even ancient Aldmer material culture.)

(Footnote: This discrepancy has been the cause of rigorous academic debate for centuries. Only this last year, 3E432, a scholar was beaten to death by his interlocutor on the streets of Rimmen, after a heated, skooma-fueled debate.)
