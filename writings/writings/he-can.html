He Can

“Someone, please!”

“My baby! She’s in there!”

The fire broke out in the dead of night. The building was old. Its tenements, already down on their luck, watched as their livelihoods, their possessions—pieces of their families—were lost to the inferno. Bystanders could hear the infant’s wails, growing harsher by the breath. The mother fought, trying to force her way past a phalanx of firefighters, clawing for the building’s sagging maw. A hell that would take her and anyone left inside with it when it fell.

The crowd parted the way they always did when they felt helpless. When there was no one left to act but those who could.

And where were they? The villains of a good life were too small for their notice. The catastrophes that ended them nothing but bygone regrets. Sad stories; endings one would never have to suffer, except in their imagining.

Hushed whispers. He waded through them. The fire reflecting on silver. Light extinguished on blue. The fear in their eyes. The awe that replaced it burned into the red sigil on his chest.

The mother saw him. Her fear was replaced too. By hope.

The whispers grew audible. One by one they realized where it came from. Hope is here.

”It’s him.”

”He’s back.”

”The Silver Savior.”

”The Blue Brave.”

”The Canned Crusader.”

He walked to the line. The fire chief nodded. The responders parted.

Then he ran, against all hesitation, dashing through the flames, stealing a fire blanket on his way into the darkness, disappearing into the inferno as it swallowed him whole. It was not courage that brought him to their aid. It was not glory. When the cola called, he had no choice but to answer.

It was a drive. A hunger. A need. He’d known it all his life.

A Thirst.

A Thirst for justice.


A support fell from the ceiling. He slid beneath it, flames licking at his aluminum skin. He jumped over another, moving like liquid. He was stronger than most. He could last. He had to. No one else had the same strength. No one else shared his burden. Three stories up, he burst into the room of the infant. He was glowing red-hot, heated to molten by the fire’s thirst. He’d known this would happen. Wrapping his arms in the blanket, as thick as he could cover them, he collected the child from her crib.

No time to waste. He jumped from the window, striking the pavement below hard enough to crack asphalt. He handed the child to her mother, turning away before she could thank him.

Then he ran—back into the blaze.

The people of this city don’t need a hero. Don’t need someone to fight the bad guys. Don’t need a symbol. They just need. Everybody needs.

He raised his hand, extinguishing flames with a burst of flavor. The door crumbled with a superpowered kick. Two children, hiding beneath the bed. Another leap, this time from the fourth story. Gratitude he never bothered to hear. More. There are more.

An old woman. A man sick in bed. A dog locked in a crate. A hamster. Each a floor higher than the rest.

Soon the entire building was cleared. Every soul saved—save one. Still he went up. He kicked open another door. The hall behind him was nothing but fire as he stepped onto the rooftop.

”I–I was going to jump. I–” The man before him was still standing on the ledge. His face stricken red with tears. Hands trembling. “I… I’m sorry. I… things have been so hard. I don’t want this anymore. Please, save me—I don’t want to die!”

The building shook. His knees. His ankles. His legs—were weak. Pain throbbed in aluminum-clad joints. There wasn’t time to climb down. He wasn’t sure he could make another jump to the ground. The buildings. He could jump to one of the buildings—try to land on one of the adjacent rooftops—

The hall behind him exploded. The man, already standing on the edge, fell backwards.

Everything narrowed to a single, pressurized point. Something bubbled in his veins. The Thirst That Cannot Be Quenched urged him forward. His body was no longer his own. All that mattered was this moment—and that he act. He ran, diving for the falling man.

He forced himself to hold the civilian tight, knowing he’d burn him. It would only be a few seconds, he told himself.

One.

Two.

Three—

The man was screaming when they struck the ground, but he was alive. He rolled out of the Canned Crusader’s arms, running for a fireman with water on hand.

His savior was left on the ground, body broken. Once-silver skin burnt black. Blue paint burnt away in wrinkled strips. The crowd gathered. Some whispering thank yous. Others wishing they could have done more. The firemen forced them back. The building was falling, exhaling dust and ash as home upon home collapsed into the closing maw.

He laid there, staring at the black sky as the fire reached for it, hungry in the same unquenchable way he was. They were one and the same, he thought. Two things brought into being by accident. Two things that couldn’t help what they were.

The world isn’t fair. That’s what people say. They don’t tell you the only way to make it better is to be unfair in the opposite direction. Being good is not a thing you’re born with. It’s a practice. An effort. A conscious decision to be what the world needs. To be thrown away when you’re used. To hate apathy till it nearly kills you.

Indifference is a thirst for meaning. I will not deprive myself of what quenches it.

A silver hand reached for the stars as they twinkled into view. The fire was dying. He was not. Right now, in that way, they were different.

He rose, ashes falling from him in sheets of grey. The red swirl on his chest revealed, untarnished. The crowd cheered. For a moment, the Thirst was lifted.

The mother came to him. He was cool now. He hadn’t realized how long he’d been lying there. She hugged him, crying into his chest as the responders tended to her child. “She’s going to live… because of you.” He wasn’t human. He wasn’t sure how one would answer.

She buried her face against his cold exterior, muffling her voice as she whispered. As she sobbed.

”God bless you, Pepsiman... God bless you...”
