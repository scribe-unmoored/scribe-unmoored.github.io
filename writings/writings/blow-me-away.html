To the young Scribe. Your imagination has taken you far.


Blow Me Away

“Mayday mayday! This is the UNSC In Due Time! We are going down! Repeat! We are going down! Requesting assistance! All lifepods ejected—we’re carrying survivors! Need immediate evac—”

The transmission cut off, just as the viewport filled with searing light. The UNSC In Due Time cracked in the center, internals swollen with purple plasma and raging fires as she split in two. Commander Faroe saw her lights flicker once, before they went out forever. Then her hull was adrift, broken and dead.

A shape pushed through the wreckage, plasma shields flaring in grids of perfect, massive hexagons. The surface of the alien ship shimmered like the scales of a fish. Like the anchorhead of a storm. Like a living thing.

”All hands! Take evasive maneuvers!”

Faroe cried over the intercom. Every man for himself, he thought as his pilots diverted the engines, squeezing from them every last ounce of thrust they could. The bridge of the UNSC Lead Left lurched. His crew was thrown sideways, artificial gravity wells too slow to respond to the sudden shift in orientation.

A storm of plasma sailed past them, launched by the alien ship. It followed in their wake, full speed ahead, missing the Lead Left by only a few thousand meters.

“Ensign, hit ‘em with the special.” Faroe called, already righted and ready to resume command.

The viewport came alight again, this time as a salvo of havoc-class nuclear warheads blossomed against the alien vessel’s starboard.

”Damage report.”

”No effect, sir. Impacted on the surface—Covies’ shields took every last drop.”

”Dammit. Get me Cutter. Arrange a boarding party. We’re not beating them in a slugfest.”

”Aye Commander.”

Faroe turned once more to the viewport. The alien ship was already out of view. The bastards were fast—so incredibly fast. It was hard to believe something so massive could move so quickly. He could see the entire fight, play by play, in his head already. The Lead Left would have enough of an angle to retreat one more time if the alien ship attempted another ram—and even then, they wouldn’t be able to swing her MAC around in time to return fire. The third pass would be their last—and they’d be dead before that if the enemy decided to open fire. Their only chance was a tactical insertion directly into the enemy vessel. A bulldog charge. It might buy just enough time to spool up the Lead Left’s slipspace drive and jump to safety. He wasn’t worried about finding volunteers—he’d find no shortage among his crew.

Overlaid on a field of dead UNSC frigates, transports, and cruisers, the visage of James Cutter, Captain of the UNSC Spirit of Fire filled the bridge with a cool, blue light. The burning sky behind him told Faroe that they were still engaged in-atmosphere. He wondered why. There was no one left to save on Arcadia—and no one to get them off.

”This is the UNSC Lead Left. Requesting Red Team’s support for a close combat operation. Captain—what are you still doing down there?”

”Negative Commander,” came Cutter’s voice. “Red Team is engaged on the ground. We’re still evacuating civilians—

”He’s not giving you three Spartans to waste on a suicide mission,” a woman’s voice chimed in, far too calm for the situation at hand. The Spirit of Fire’s shipboard artificial intelligence, Serena, finished the Captain’s answer for him. Faroe couldn’t see her—but he was certain she could see him.

Cutter was still evacuating? Half the damn fleet was in ruins above Arcadia.The Spirit was one hell of a ship—but did the Captain really have that much faith in her? He hoped for the sake of Cutter’s crew, and the civilians he must have been onboarding, that his trust was well-placed.

Faroe sighed. “Understood, Captain.” He made sure the A.I. could hear the second part. “I’ll make you a deal—get your people out of there alive—”

”And in return?” Serena interrupted him this time. He could hear the smile in her voice.

”Make sure you get a picture of us kicking ass on your way out.”

Cutter gave a dry, exhausted laugh. “Deal.” was the only thing he said before the channel was closed.

Somewhere distant, Faroe could hear his crew shouting as the alien vessel readied itself for another pass. Still, they hadn’t opened fire. The bastards were toying with them. He snapped back into his command chair–back into his own head. He gave the order for evasive maneuvers once more, this time braced and ready.

There was no helping it. Getting a clean jump was already a longshot. Knowing there were still some civilians to evacuate—that the losses they’d suffered today might actually mean something—

”UNSC Lead Left, this is your captain speaking. Take one last look at the people around you. They are giving up their lives to save what’s left of the innocent men, women, and children on the rock below us. Humanity will remember the fall of Arcadia. You are buying her time so that your children can launch one hell of a counteroffensive. In the meantime—boarding party standby. We’re taking as many with us as we can.”

There was quiet, grim assent from the bridge crew.

When the intercom was closed, he turned to the console nearest to him, a silver glow of light projecting from an emitter at its apex. Faroe focused his attention on it, a second later, the miniature avatar was of a woman with curled hair, clad in a heavy, draping peplos appeared. “Hypatia.” The Commander said to her. “Get the Spartan out of the brig. He’s volunteering to go with them.

The AI nodded in answer, before disappearing to relay his command. Faroe slumped back into his chair, whispering to himself. ”Last one out, turn out the lights.”

The alien ship flew past, missing them for the second, and final time.

***

The armory was quick to put him back in MJOLNIR. That much told him he wasn’t being freed willingly. Faroe had plans for him—and probably nothing good. Three weeks rotting in that damn cell. Pulling him out so soon only meant one thing: Faroe needed firepower. Disposable firepower.

A piece of Smiley-118 was almost grateful.

That wasn’t his real name, of course. But neither was 118. He decided a long time ago he might as well change the one that mattered.

”Gordon,” the AI began. “Do you require a sitrep—your vitals indicate you might not be aware of what Commander Faroe has asked of you.”

Asked. Interesting choice of words. Before the war, the UNSC was never much for asking. Now with the Covenant at humanity’s throat, they could ask whatever they wanted. “Negative, Hyp. I know what this is about.” He didn’t bother correcting her on his name—Hypatia had always been better at arguing.

”Very well, one-one-eight. Goodluck—and Godspeed.”

He stepped out onto the evac deck. The lifepods were still completetly intact. Faroe knew there was no point attempting to fire them. The Covenant had no qualms about shooting them down. A contingent of marines, military engineers, and civilian contractors were mustered near the closest. An officer was briefing them on the mission—a boarding, clearly. Smiley took his place by the only other active pod—that would be his. No sense in crushing everyone onboard in case they hit turbulence. Two pods it was.

They were loaded shortly after. Time was of the essence, apparently. Smiley was aboard his pod, staring out into the nothingness of space, when they were launched. The pod soared through the inky blackness, shot parallel to the Left. Weird heading, he thought to himself. They left the ship behind, a stretch of void the only thing connecting them. His mouth dropped open inside his helmet when he saw that the space between them was filled with a dozen warships—and twice as many civilian vessels.

”E.M.P. ready, Spartan. Lock your armor.”

”Wait—E.M.P.? Hyp, what’s going—”

In an instant, the doors to the pod swung open, venting violently as the atmosphere was sucked into the vacuum of space.

Smiley clung to a railing. A second later, and the Left exploded into cinders behind them, crushed headlong by the Covenant ship.

Another second later, and the E.M.P. activated—just as they struck the alien vessel’s energy shields.

Smiley locked his armor just in time.

He saw the vacuum of space. The flaring alien shields as the E.M.P. knocked them offline. The enemy hangar’s threshold. Purple alloy and blue holograms. A mist of purple blood as his rigid body—nearly half a ton clad in his MJOLNIR armor—vaporized some poor alien bastard.

Smiley was on his feet before he stopped moving, slamming an armored fist through the alien alloy beneath his feet. He came to a screeching halt, feet away from flying back into the void of space.

”Hyp! Hypatia! You still with me?!”

A chirp announced the AI’s answer. “Affirmative, Spartan. Commander Faroe transmitted me to your armor before the Left’s demise. Said, ‘I would be better use to you alive than dead.’”

He allowed himself a disbelieving chuckle. ”You’ve executed the Cole Protocol?”

”Yes. All references to [REDACTED] have been wiped. We are green for close combat.”

Good.

I need a weapon.
