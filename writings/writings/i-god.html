I, God

Heduanna was a young woman, called beautiful by her goddess Ananna. She had dark hair and bronze skin, and this morning she wore a simple tunic of flax.

Today she was going to the market. Ananna reminded her they were in need of textile; Heduanna’s father had rent a garment while laboring. Ananna deemed it would be filial to mend it for him.

Heduanna trusted Ananna more than anyone else, and so she listened. From her earliest days, the goddess was her guide. Ananna’s voice was soft and sweet. They would soothe Heduanna with her favorite songs and dancing images of her favorite memories. Ananna was an excellent judge, keeping Heduanna safe always.

The city of Ur was unlike anything else in the world, Heduanna’s father had once said. Nowhere else did people have walls and soldiers to protect them. The people from beyond had no temples—or at least, none that lasted. Heduanna wondered how they spoke to their gods without temples. She’d never lived anywhere except the city. How could the gods be happy without a temple to rest in? Ananna was always happiest at the base of the Ziggurat, where the people gathered in the market. There traders came from distant lands, bringing new things, new sights, and new smells for Heduanna and Ananna to enjoy.

Ananna reminded Heduanna to retrieve a satchel of barley for the trade, before they set off to the Ziggurat. Heduanna thanked them—she’d almost forgotten.

At the market, Heduanna found the textile with Ananna’s help, quickly finding their friend, Gemekala. Gemekala was a seamstress, and had offered to teach Heduanna in the dry season before last. Ananna had urged her to take the lessons. Ever since, the woman had been a good friend of the girl and her goddess.

“Good morning,” Heduanna smiled at the woman. Ananna told her to be careful of her tone—Gemekala seemed distant, preoccupied.

“Good morning,” Gemekala looked at her all too suddenly, surprised but not unhappy that the pair had come. Heduanna traded the barley for a bolt of textile, reminded by Ananna to observe all her manners. She thanked Gemekala, and made to leave.

But Ananna was concerned for their friend, and urged Heduanna to ask Gemekala what was the matter.

“Oh, nothing sweet girl. Nammu is being stubborn.” The seamstress’ head made little circles, as though her god had thrown her off balance. Even so, she smiled sweetly—until she had a sudden thought. “Heduanna, I heard the strangest riddle the other day, and I fear Nammu is upset with me, for they did not know the answer.”

That was not uncommon, Heduanna thought. Ananna was terrible at riddles—as was she. “What was the riddle?” Heduanna asked.

The seamstress opened her mouth to speak, stopped herself, and began again. “Who is it that answers when I speak to you, Heduanna?”

“Well, Ananna and I, of course.”

The seamstress considered the girl’s answer, then continued. “And who hears Ananna when she speaks?”

Heduanna stared at her. She thought for a long time. Certainly it was not Gemekala who heard Ananna—Heduanna could not hear Nammu, after all. The answer—though obvious—seemed strange, in a way.

“I do, I suppose.” She admitted at last.

“That was my answer as well, when I was asked,” the seamstress said with a relieved smile. “These lousy copper merchants, bringing us their riddles. They ought to stay beyond the walls, with the other troublesome things.”

They had a laugh about the whole ordeal, said their goodbyes, and parted ways.

Heduanna thought she should stay a while longer in the shadow of the Ziggurat, savoring its shade while the sun was at its height. She found a beetle, chased it with a stick for a time, and—when the day grew cool—she decided to go home.

She sewed her father’s clothes quickly and presented it to him as a gift when he arrived home. He was a jovial man, always delighted by her kindness. Extolling his daughter for her considerate nature, he told her that his Anu was pleased with her, and that she and Ananna were blessed.

Heduanna was happy to see her father’s smile. At the mention of her goddess, she listened for Ananna. Somehow, she hadn’t realized how quiet they’d grown.

Ananna hadn't spoken since she answered the riddle. Strange, Heduanna thought.

Perhaps they were resting.
