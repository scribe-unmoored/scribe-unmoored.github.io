I, God

Heduanna was a young woman, called beautiful by her goddess Ananna. She had dark hair and bronze skin, and this morning she wore a simple tunic of flax.

Today she was going to the market. Ananna reminded her they were in need of textile; Heduanna’s father had rent a garment while laboring. Ananna, always offering her the best advice, deemed it would be filial to mend it.

Heduanna trusted the goddess more than anyone else, and so she listened. From her earliest days, Ananna was her guide. Whenever she was lonely, the goddess’ voice was soft and sweet, always there to keep her company. They would soothe Heduanna with her favorite songs and dancing images of her favorite memories. Ananna was an excellent judge. They always kept the girl safe.

They lived together with Heduanna's family and their gods, in the city of Ur. Heduanna's father had once said it was unlike anything else in the world—nowhere else did people have walls and soldiers to protect them. Nowhere in the world had a Ziggurat has grand as theirs. The people from beyond had no temples at all—or at least, none that lasted. Heduanna wondered how they spoke to their gods without temples. After all, Ananna was always happiest in the shadow of the Great Temple. How could one's god be happy without a temple to rest in? Ananna was always happiest at the base of the Ziggurat—as was Heduanna. There the people gathered in the market. The traders came from distant lands, bringing new things, new sights, and new smells for the girl and her goddess to enjoy.

Ananna reminded Heduanna to retrieve a satchel of barley, before they set off to the Ziggurat. They would not be able to purchase the textile without it. Heduanna thanked her goddess—she’d almost forgotten.

At the market, they quickly found their friend, Gemekala, who was a seamstress. She was older, with lines worn into her bronze face by the labor of the sun. Her stall, burdened by bolts of patterned textiles and a hefty folio of sewing needles, was shaded beneath a cloth tarpaulin—one that Heduanna had helped the seamstress sew herself. Once, the Gemekala had offered to teach the girl the skill of sewing, in the dry season before last. Ananna, always offering the best advice, urged her to take the opportunity. Ever since, the girl and her goddess had been good friends with Gemekala—and her god, Nammu.

“Good morning,” Heduanna smiled at the seamstress. Ananna told her to be careful of her tone—Gemekala seemed distant, preoccupied by some troubling thought.

“Good morning,” Gemekala looked at her too suddenly. She seemed surprised—but not unhappy that the pair had come. The trade was pleasant and quick. Ananna always made sure Heduanna observed all her manners. Gemekala, though she was a friend, was an elder as well. Heduanna thanked her when the trade was done, and made to leave.

But Ananna was concerned for their friend, and urged Heduanna to ask her what thought was so troubling.

“Oh, nothing sweet girl. Nammu is being stubborn.” The seamstress’ head made little circles as she answered, as though her god had thrown her off balance. Even so, she smiled sweetly—until she seemed to have a sudden thought. “It is strange. I heard the most odd riddle the other day, and I fear Nammu is upset with me, for they did not know the answer.”

It not uncommon for a god to be fooled by riddles, Heduanna thought. Ananna was always terrible at riddles—as was she, though it never stopped them from trying to answer. “What was the riddle?” Heduanna asked. Her goddess was eager to solve it.

The seamstress opened her mouth to speak, stopped herself, and began again. She spoke carefully, as though the riddle was difficult to recall. “Who is it that answers when I speak to you, Heduanna?”

“Well, Ananna and I, of course.”

The seamstress considered the girl’s answer, then continued. She had no trouble repeating the second question. “And who hears Ananna when she speaks?”

Heduanna stared at her. For a long time she could say nothing. Certainly it was not the seamstress who heard Ananna when they spoke—Heduanna could not hear Nammu, after all. Ananna nearly offered a reply, but then withdrew. The answer—though it was obvious—felt peculiar to say aloud. Heduanna did not know why.

“I do, I suppose.” She admitted at last.

“That was my answer as well, when I was asked,” the seamstress said with a relieved chuckle. “These lousy copper merchants, bringing us their riddles. They ought to stay beyond the walls, with the other troublesome things.”

They both shared a laugh about the whole ordeal. What sort of riddle had such simple answers? They talked until a new customer approached, whereupon they said their goodbyes and parted ways.

Heduanna thought she should stay a while longer in the shadow of the Ziggurat, savoring its shade while the sun was at its height. She found a beetle, chased it with a stick for a time, and—when the day grew cool—she decided to go home.

She sewed her father’s clothes quickly, presenting it as a gift to him when he returned home. He was a jovial man, though he labored beneath the sun all day, working with the other men to strengthen the walls of Ur. He was always delighted by her gifts, though, and his smile always made her happy. Extolling his daughter for her considerate nature, he told her that his Anu was pleased with her, and that she and Ananna were blessed.

At the mention of her goddess, Heduanna listened for them; but Ananna had grown quiet.

Strange, Heduanna thought.

Perhaps they were resting.
