Chapter I: The Blade

4th of Morning Star, 3E405

Two hours past midnight

High Rock

Drip. Drip. Drip.

The castaway woke with a gasp.

Every breath burnt. Every effort agony. It was a long time before he could still himself. He coughed, his lungs still full of water.

Blinking, he willed his eyes to open, squeezing them shut, stretching them open. Fingers shaking, tracing the lines of his open eyelids, he realized they already were.

Nothing. Not even a hint of shadow passed through his sight.

Have I gone blind?

He pressed his hand to his chest, where the pain was most intense. A fresh sting of pain. Something hung loose there, the bone retreating at his touch. There should have been a warm wash of blood over his hand. He should have felt the cold wetness of his skin. Instead, there was nothing. His fingers were numb. The linens he wore clung to him like the hands of the grave. He shivered, rubbing his hands together, trying desperately to bring feeling back. The sound echoed in the darkness.

Slowly, his breath slowed. The pain began to subside. There was no room for panic now. No further room for error. He listened. Past his pain and fear. The only sound in this place was the pounding in his head—and the quiet dribble of falling water.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

His memory drifted back to him in pieces. A storm. A cave-in.

The mission.

Had any beside him survived? Was there anyone else to carry it forward? For a moment, lying in what must have been a pool of his own, cold blood, he hoped another had. Someone to take his place. Someone to take this burden from him at last.

Then he cursed himself for his cowardice.

You've survived worse. You will not die in this place.

Get up. You are a Blade of the Emperor.

Get. Up.

He reached out for something to steady himself, willing himself to stand. When his hand met only air, he stood anyway, forcing through pain that should have crucified him against the stone.

The world was swimming, spinning about inside his head like a bowl of cold soup. The ground turned traitor beneath his feet, spinning opposite the sloshing in his skull. He could see nothing. No walls. No floor. Nothing to tell him which way was up.

Drip. Drip. Splash.

Before he could fall again, he lurched, doubling over, emptying a stomach full of seawater—along with that morning’s breakfast.

”Fetcher…” The castaway wiped the corner of his mouth. The smell of salt and iron and sick drifted up at him from the cave floor.

”I need to leave this place,” he said aloud. The cave had only one answer:

Drip. Drip. Drip.

He took a shambling step forward, then another, and another. Step by step warmth returned to him, though he was still cold. Intolerably cold. There was no wind in that place. No sound of life or living things. The cold air passed through him as though he was a ghost, an eidolon in his own body. The pounding in his skull was the only evidence to the contrary.

He took another step, kicking something metal with his bare foot. What followed was a familiar, resounding ring. Steel. The castaway bent as far as he could tolerate, wooden fingers searching for the hilt of his sword. He kicked something wooden—the scabbard. He picked it up. The weapon inside fell to the ground, broken into a thousand invisible fragments. The sound they made, an storm of echoes in what had once been silence, made him wince. They announced announced to the prowling dark where he lie within, weak and dying. He reached to the ground when all grew silent again, fingers brushing the ruined blade.

The weight was wrong in his hand. Too heavy in the hilt. He ran his thumb along its edge, touch light as air as he followed its blade. It ended suddenly—too suddenly—in a jagged, broken point. He cursed again.

A sword of Akaviri make ought to outlast its wielder. This one had not.

He sheathed the weapon. He would not leave it behind. To do so would be to betray what he was. His oath. The life he'd carried for nearly six decades.

And besides, a jagged shard of steel was still just that. A weapon.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

The ceiling of the cave was as black as the world around him. Since his childhood, he'd watched the stars. Here, he could not see those that would determine his fate. Even hidden from its sight, he could feel the Atronach burning in his soul. If he had any magicka left, it would be enough for a spark—enough to light a fire. A spell or two. He cursed the sign of his birth, trudging onward. The pain in his side stole away his breath with every heavy, limping step.

He could remember more clearly now.

The mainstay was shattered beneath the storm's wrath. It came from nowhere, like an angry thing woken from fragile slumber. He could hear the cries of the crew. Men thrown overboard. Some crushed as the ship was split in two. He'd been thrown as well. There was a rush of cold seawater. The taste of salt and iron still lingered in his mouth. He'd had time enough to grab his sword and naught else. The tides carried him to this cave, half-drowned and exhausted. He could remember crawling ashore, every muscle aching in protest, exhaustion threatening to pull him back beneath the waves. By some cruel trick of fate, the cave began to collapse. He remembered stumbling to his feet. Running.

Then darkness.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

He brought a shaking hand to his face. He knew it was there, though still he could not see it. He flexed his fingers. Still numb.

Need to get warm. Need to see—if I still can. His hand remembered the shape of a spell, fingers curling to cradle a fledgling flame. Small. Fragile. Like an infant's heartbeat, held in the palm of his hand.

The flow of magicka began somewhere far above, passing through sky and earth—from somewhere behind the stars. A place he'd never been, but somewhere familiar all the same. Aetherius. It flowed through his temples, his chest, pooling in the palm of his hand. Once it had come to him in a raging torrent. Now, there was little more than a dying stream.

Even in the deepest darkness, it took only the smallest fire to bring light. When the flame flickered to life, he could almost feel the darkness melt away; drawn from his lungs like venom from a wound, the oppressive weight on his chest lifted. Finally, he could see. He chuckled in disbelief. There was no humor in it. The effort renewed the sharp pain in his side.

Not blind then.

The ground ran red. A pool of blood—far too much blood—gathered at his feet. Ahead, the Blade could see a log of driftwood and an abandoned firepit. Beyond that, a rotting wooden door, leading deeper. He turned back. The sea and sky were gone, hidden behind an avalanche of rubble, sealed by mud and sand. There was only one way out now.

Limping, he made his way to the log, setting himself against it. There was no way of knowing if he was alone in this place, only that if he did not get warm, or cauterize his wounds, he would die long before another could claim his life.

He slung the flame into the firepit, sliding to sit against the log. His head fell back, resting against a smooth, sea-worn knot. The log was immense. Large enough to offer protection. Ancient enough that even the sea had not yet worn it to nothing. The Blade drew his broken sword as he laid against it, placed the scabbard in his mouth, and bit down. The fire began with embers. He helped coax it to life with a whispered word. Warmth washed over him as it began to swell.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

He could feel the feeling return to his hands. Something ran down his cheek, warm, carrying the scent of iron. He raised a hand to his brow, pressing against the pounding in his skull. The warmth coated it, running between his fingers with every pulse of his heart.

He was no healer. The only school of magic he knew was Destruction. His only arcana fire.

The wound was great. He'd lost too much blood.

Need to...

He steeled himself. Hand pressed to the gash in his head, he called on the stream of magicka once more. Enough for one more.

Drip. Dr—

He cried out as the flesh was seared shut.

The dripping stopped.
