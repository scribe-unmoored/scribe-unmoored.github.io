Chapter I: The Blade

4th of Morning Star, 3E405

Two hours past midnight

High Rock

Drip. Drip. Drip.

The castaway woke with a gasp.

Every breath burnt. Every effort agony. It was a long time before he could still himself. He coughed, his lungs still full of water.

Blinking, he willed his eyes to open, squeezing them shut, stretching them open. Fingers shaking, tracing the lines of his open eyelids, he realized they already were.

Nothing. Not even the hint of a shadow passed through his sight.

Have I gone blind?

He pressed his hand to his chest, where the pain was most intense. A fresh sting of pain. Something loose in his chest. There should have been a warm wash of blood over his hand. The cold wetness of his skin. Instead, there was nothing. His fingers were numb. The linens he wore clung to him like the hands of the grave. He shivered, rubbing his hands together, trying desperately to bring feeling back to them. The sound echoed in the darkness.

Slowly, his breath slowed. The pain began to subside. There was no room for panic now. No further room for error. He listened. Past his pain and fear. The only sound in this place was the pounding in his head—and the quiet dribble of falling water.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

His memory drifted back to him in pieces. A storm. A cave-in.

The mission.

Had any beside him survived? Was there anyone else to carry it forward? For a moment, lying in what must have been a pool of his own, cold blood, he hoped another had. Someone to take his place. Someone to take this burden from him at last.

Then he cursed himself for his cowardice.

You've survived worse. You will not die in this place.

Get up. You are a Blade of the Emperor.

Get. Up.

He reached out for something to steady himself, willing himself to stand. When his hand met only air, he stood anyway, forcing through pain that should have crucified him against the stone.

The world was swimming, spinning about inside his head like a bowl of cold soup. The ground turned traitor beneath his feet, spinning opposite the sloshing in his skull. He could see nothing. No walls. No floor. Nothing to tell him which way was up.

Drip. Drip. Splash.

Before he could fall again, he lurched, doubling over, emptying a stomach full of seawater. Along with that morning’s breakfast.

”Fetcher…” The castaway wiped the corner of his mouth. The smell of salt and iron and sick drifted up at him from the cave floor.

”I need to leave this place,” he said aloud. The cave had only one answer:

Drip. Drip. Drip.

He took a shambling step forward, then another, and another. Step by step warmth returned to him, though he was still cold. Intolerably cold. There was no wind in that place. No sound of life or living things. The cold air passed through him as though he was a ghost, an eidolon in his own body. The pounding in his skull was the only evidence to the contrary.

He took another step, kicking something metal with his bare foot. The resounding ring that followed was familiar. Steel. The castaway bent as far as he could tolerate, wooden fingers searching for the hilt of his sword. He kicked something wooden—the scabbard. He picked it up, the weapon inside falling to the ground, broken into a thousand invisible fragments. The sound they made as they fell was intolerable, a storm of echoes fighting one another to be the loudest. To announced to the prowling dark where he lie within it, weak and dying. He reached to the ground as all grew silent again, fingers brushing the ruined blade.

The weight was wrong in his hand. Too heavy in the hilt. He ran his thumb along its edge, touch light as air as he followed its blade. It ended suddenly—too suddenly—in a jagged, broken point. He cursed again.

A sword of Akaviri make ought to outlast its wielder. This one had not.

He sheathed the weapon. He would not leave it behind. To do sowould be to betray what he was. His oath. The life he'd carried for nearly six decades.

And besides, a jagged shard of steel was still just that. A weapon.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

The ceiling of the cave was as black as the world around him. Since his childhood, he'd watched the stars. Here, he could not see those that would determine his fate. Even hidden from its sight, he could feel the Atronach burning in his soul. If he had any magicka left, it would be enough for a spark—enough to light a fire, perhaps. He cursed the sign of his birth, trudging onward. The pain in his side stole away his breath with every heavy, limping step.

He remembered more clearly now.

The mainstay was shattered beneath the storm's wrath. It came from nowhere, like an angry thing woken from fragile slumber. He could hear the cries of the crew. Men thrown overboard. Some crushed as the ship was split in two. He'd been thrown overboard, having time enough to grab his sword and naught else. The tides carried him to this cave. He remembered crawling ashore, exhausting threatening to pull him back beneath the waves. The cave had begun to collapse. He remembered stumbling to his feet. Running.

Then darkness.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

He brought a shaking hand to his face. He knew it was there, though still he could not see it. He flexed his fingers. Still numb. His hand remembered the shape of a spell, curling to house a fledgling flame. Small. Fragile. Like an infant's heartbeat, held in the palm of his hand.

The flow of magicka began somewhere far above, passing through sky and earth—from somewhere behind the stars. It flowed through his temples, his chest, pooling in the palm of his hand. Once it had come to him in a raging torrent. Now, there was little more than a dying stream.

The fire lit the cave. Finally, he could see. He chuckled in disbelief. The effort it took renewed the sharp pain in his side.

Not blind then.

The ground ran red. A pool of blood—far too much blood—gathered at his feet. Ahead, the Blade could see a log of driftwood and an abandoned firepit. Beyond that, a rotting wooden door, leading deeper into the unknown.

Limping, he made his way to the log, setting himself against it. There was no way of knowing if he was alone in this place, only that if he did not get warm, nor cauterize his wounds, he would die long before another could claim his life.

He slung the flame into the firepit, sliding to sit against the log. His head fell back, resting against a smooth, sea-worn knot. The log was immense. Large enough to offer protection, and ancient enough that even the sea had not yet worn it to nothing. The Blade drew his sword as he laid against it. He cast the flame into the firepit, coaxing it to life with a whispered word. Warmth washed over him as it began to swell.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

He could feel the feeling return to his hands. Something ran down his cheek, warm, carrying the scent of iron. He raised a hand to his brow, pressing against the pounding in his skull. The warmth coated it, running between his fingers with every pulse of his heart.

He was no healer. The only school of magic he knew was Destruction. His only arcana fire.

The wound was great. He'd lost too much blood.

He steeled himself. Hand pressed to the gash in his head, he called on the stream of magicka once more.

Drip. Dr—

And the dripping stopped.
