Chapter I: Privateer's Hold

The castaway woke in darkness, head throbbing from where he’d been struck. The last thing he could remember was the way the mainstay cracked. For a second, there had been something louder than the storm. Louder than the voices of the sailors screaming. Louder than the oil-fire that raged to spite the rains. Then there was no sound at all—nothing except the muffled cry of a ship breaking, and the taste of salt filling his mouth and nostrils.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

Now it was dark. The sound of falling water and crumbling rock was the only discernible thing in a sea of void. The ground beneath him was hard and cold. He tried pressing against it, willing himself to stand. His arms shook with weakness, before giving in to the futility of the effort. When he fell against the stone once more, there was no pain. He’d barely fallen an inch, and besides, there was no feeling in his face. The castaway flexed his hands. They felt like wood.

There were stories, he remembered. Stories of where a man went when he died. Some said Aetherius was like waking up to blinding, searing light. Then you never felt anything else again. For many, that was the preferable end. Others spoke of Oblivion—where the Daedric Princes rule. The castaway listened for some hint that he hadn’t awoken in one of those terrifying realms—but all was silent. There was no crumble of brimstone, nor the soft clink of chains. If he strained hard enough, he thought he could hear the sound of wolves howling—but that must have been impossible. The landslide that knocked him unconscious had buried him deep.

There had been a landslide, he could recall suddenly. He’d found his way into this seaside cave. There had been just enough time to light a fire before the entrance collapsed. It had nearly buried him. He was running for a door at the far end when everything went black.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

The castaway shivered. The cold had reached his bones by now. His clothes were soaked through. "Fetcher, he swore to no one.

He dragged himself to the edge of his dead fire. It had been smothered beneath a torrent of mud and rock. Reaching deep for a spell of fire, he could feel the waters of his magicka swell. In his mind’s eye, he could see a stream; the waters came suddenly, flowing around a rock in the center of the stream. That was how a battlemage was taught to see the shape of magic—the way energies hummed across the soul. At the height of his power, he could see a raging river, his soul a stone hidden beneath the black waters. His spells would be like the fires of Red Mountain then. Now, all the castaway could feel was a trickle—and barely even that.

The cave lit with a spark at the tip of his finger. It jumped to a sprig of dry kindling. By some miracle, the fire smoldered, crackled, and then rose lazily to life. There was blood on the ground. Far too much blood. The cave was limestone, with stalactites longer than man’s leg hanging threateningly at the edge of the firelight above. There was a log of driftwood, abdicated by the sea long ago. He dragged himself to it, placing himself between the wood and the fire. The wooden scabbard at his side scraped the cave floor as he did.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

Sitting up against the log, the castaway fought with his sword. Drawing the weapon should have been easy, but when it finally came free, only a piece of the blade followed the hilt into the firelight. The castaway caught the gleam of his eyes—red like the blood on the ground—on the face of the broken blade. It was little more than a jagged piece of metal now. Without the scabbard, one would have no way of appreciating the subtle curved edge the sword once carried. The castaway swore again, this time at himself. A blade of Akaviri make ought to outlast its wielder. This one had not.

The fire had at last begun to warm him. He let his thumb trace the edge of the ruined katana. It was still sharp. The broken tip still ended in a jagged point. Good. Oblivion or no, a shoto is better than no blade at all. As he began to stow the weapon in its scabbard once more, he noticed the wrappings on the hilt were undone. The guarskin was rough on his fingers—fingers that could finally feel again—as he unwrapped it. Seeing that the tang and wooden handle beneath were intact, the castaway heaved a sigh of relief. My weapon is as I am; wounded, not dead. The blade could be reforged, if he could make it back to a smith of his order. That would be a long journey—if he could even obtain permission to make it. Cloud Ruler Temple was a long way away from the Iliac Bay. In any case, it would need to wait. His mission was here. He was still alive, despite that monster of a storm. He sheathed the sword with a word of thanks, lamenting that it would have to wait.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

He felt something warm running down his face. His hand made its way to his brow. The warmth coated his palm, and the dripping stopped.
