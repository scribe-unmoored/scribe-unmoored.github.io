Chapter I: Privateer's Hold

4th of Morning Star

Third Era, 405

Two hours past midnight

Drip. Drip. Drip.

The castaway woke with a gasp.

He shuddered, stilling himself, willing his breath to slow. His lungs, still full of water, convulsed violently. He began to cough. His chest felt as though it was being torn open with every spasm.

Blinking, he willed his eyes to open. No matter the effort, it was as though they were sewn shut. He squeezed them, forced them open, and waved his hand before his face.

Nothing. Not even the hint of a shadow passed through his sight.

Have I gone blind?

Every breath burnt. Every movement was agony. He pressed his hand to his chest, where the pain was most intense. One of his ribs moved when it should not have. There was no warm wash of blood over his hand. He could feel nothing at all, in fact. His fingers were numb, as was his cheek—cold where it had been pressed against the cave floor. The linens he wore clung to him like the hands of the grave. He shivered, and tried desperately to bring feeling back to his hands. Rubbing them together helped a little. The scuffling sound echoed through the darkness.

Slowly, his breathing grew tolerable. He listened. Past his pain and fear. The only sound in this place was the pounding in his head—and the sound of falling water.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

In the silence, his memory drifted back to him in pieces. A storm. Seawater. A cave-in. His mission. The shame of dying in this cave—of failing to complete it—quieted all else for but a moment. He reached out for something to steady himself, willing himself to stand.

When his hand met only air, he forced himself to stand anyway.

”Fetcher!” he swore, groaning as he rose. The world was swimming, spinning about inside his head like a bowl of cold soup. The ground turned traitor beneath his feet, spinning opposite the sloshing in his skull. He could see nothing. No walls. No floor. Nothing to tell him which way was up.

Drip. Drip. Splash.

Before he could fall again, he lurched, doubling over. He emptied a stomach full of seawater onto the stone, along with that morning’s breakfast.

”Fetcher…” The castaway wiped the corner of his mouth. The smell of salt and iron and sick drifted up at him from the cave floor.

”I need to leave this place,” he said aloud. There was no one else but the darkness to hear. The cave had only one answer:

Drip. Drip. Drip.

He took a shambling step forward, and another. With some effort, he could quiet his wounds. His breathing was still strained, but he found that standing grew easier by the moment. He took another step and something metal struck his bare foot. He remembered very suddenly what it was. The castaway bent as far as he could tolerate, wooden fingers searching for the hilt of his sword. He kicked something wooden—the scabbard. The sound of steel ringing against stone was intolerable as he picked it up. Still, now he knew where the sword lay, and reached for it.

The weight was wrong in his hand. Too heavy in the hilt. He brought his thumb against its edge, his touch light as air as he scanned its blade. It ended too suddenly—in a jagged, ruined point. He cursed again. A sword of Akaviri make ought to outlast its wielder. This one had not. He returned it to its scabbard, and the scabbard to his belt. To leave the weapon behind would be to betray what he was—and besides, the shard of jagged steel was still just that. A weapon.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

The ceiling of the cave was as black as the world around him. Here, he could not see the stars that determined his fate. If he had any magicka left, it would be enough for a spark—enough to light a fire, perhaps. He cursed the Atronach as he trudged onward. The pain in his side stole away his breath with every heavy, limping step.

He was a Blade. A servant of the Empire. He could remember clearly now. He would not let himself die in a place like this.

The sword at his side was the symbol of his oath. The void of magicka in his soul was the Atronach’s blessing. He could see in his mind’s eye the shattering of the mainstay; hear the cries of the crew as men were thrown overboard. The storm roared. Not with the thunder of some lesser tempest—but like some living thing. Like the dragons of old. The tides carried him to this cave, where he had only time to crawl across the limestone before it began to collapse. He remembered stumbling to his feet. Running. And then darkness.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

He grimaced against his pain, and brought a shaking hand to his face. He knew it was there, though still he could not see it.

Somewhere from behind the stars, the trickle of a stream flowed. That was how a mage knew magicka. Once, that trickle had been a torrent. Now, his energies emaciated, there was almost nothing left to call upon. His hand remembered the shape of the spell. The cave lit beneath the glow of a feeble flame, set like a heartbeat in his hand.

He allowed himself a disbelieving chuckle when he realized he was not blind.

The relief did not last. The ground was red with blood, mingling with seawater and shards of ruined steel. Ahead, the Blade could see a log of driftwood, and an abandoned firepit. Beyond that, a rotting wooden door, leading deeper into the unknown.

Limping, he made his way to the log, setting himself against it. He cried out as pain tore through his side again. The echo was loud—loud enough that any enemy near enough would hear. He wasn’t worried about that now. If he did not get warm, nor cauterize his wounds, he would die long before another could claim his life.

He slung the flame into the firepit, sliding to his rear against the log. His head fell back, resting against a smooth, sea-worn knot. The log was immense. Large enough to offer protection, and ancient enough that even the sea had not yet worn it to nothing. The Blade drew his sword as he laid against it, gasping for air. The warmth of the fire washed over him as it swelled to life.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

He could feel the feeling return to his hands. Something warm ran down his cheek. He raised a hand to his brow, pressing against the pounding in his skull. The warmth coated it, running between his fingers with every pulse of his heart.

The dripping stopped.
