Chapter II: The Lady

11th of Morning Star

Third Era, 405

Midday, City of Wayrest

The city of Wayrest stretched to the horizon, where it met the glimmering waters of the Iliac Bay. Ashlar homes decorated the townships, placed further from the palace than the red-roofed market quarter, where Levoline had passed through the day prior, observing all manner of shops, taverns, and guildhalls swollen with sellswords and minstrels. The crowds flowed through the streets like lifeblood, even in the distant reaches of the city. The walls had been built and rebuilt over centuries of growth. Levoline imagined that if one could see as a dragon, Wayrest would look like the stump of a great tree, hewn at the roots.

Alas, that the dragons were no more.

Her own reflection stared back at her, as though a stranger had caught her gaze from the other side of the window. The woman was dressed in the attire of Eadwyre’s court—an ensemble she never would have chosen for herself, Levoline knew. No high elf would. Her dress was green brocade silk. Which, though elegant in itself, was marred by ostentatious trimmings. Sleeves that she felt broke the silhouette of her tall, elegant form. Tassels which dragged the ground, gathering dust from every corner of the palace. A simple gown—even the brocade she was wearing—was far more befitting an Altmer noblewoman. The only parts of her she could still recognize were the pricks of her golden ears, peeking from beneath a curtain of auburn hair. Her eyes, “green like chrysoprase,” as her father had always said, were the only thing in the reflection that reminded Levoline who the stranger was.

“Consider it to be in the interests of diplomacy,” her retainer had said. “The Bretons of High Rock’s courts are as demanding as the sapiarchs, and prone thricefold to treachery.”

Diplomacy, she’d scoffed. Would the concession be worth it? she wondered. It was one thing to feign deference—Levoline could use that, she knew. It was another to demonstrate submission. Her faith in her retainer had not increased after he’d suggested the vestments. Uuelmo had only been a courtier of Eadwyre’s for two decades—not nearly the experience she’d hoped for on a mission like this. The only informant even close enough to Eadwyre to proffer her an invitation to his court had been stationed there only twenty years prior—and only to investigate an inventory of arcane goods lost to smuggling. Uuelmo, though he’d maintained an air of composure the last two days, was certainly just as underequipped for the task at hand as she was. After all, Levoline herself was only one hundred and twelve years of age—and still little more than a fledgling of House Morimacil. Uuelmo, though nearly a century her elder, seemed little older than she.

”I require your answer on the morrow. We cannot delay.” Uuelmo said to her. His voice interrupted her thoughts, pulling her attention away from the window, where she’d been surveying the city below, and into the cramped chambers of his office.

Uuelmo stood opposite the desk from her, on the same side she’d been on only yesterday. Though he was an Altmer, Levoline thought him only just tall enough to be considered stately, with a face so evenly divided that his nose, brow, and chin comprised it in equal portions. He too was clad in the garb of a Breton courtier—a quilted doublet over a suede shirt, with hosen to match. An ugly form of dress, she thought to herself. Inelegant and gaudy, like her own clothes.

”You will not have to wait that long,” she answered him right away. She’d made up her mind on the voyage from Alinor.

”I understand you are eager to begin, Lady Levoline. But the court of Wayrest is no place for temerity. Here there is no shortage of brigands or savages, and not all of them outside the palace walls. If you accept this assignment, you alone will be responsible for your failure. No one may know that you are working for us.”

”I am aware, ser.” Levoline’s hand came to rest on the hilt of her rapier—a habit she had yet to rid herself of, even after decades of courtly discipline. It was as though her hand found Thorn of its own volition. Quickly, she feigned that she was only brushing the pommel, and let her hand drift to her side. Uuelmo gave no hint that she’d been convincing. “I wish to serve. As my father, and his father before him.”

“No doubt you do, my lady. No doubt you do. House Morimacil is renowned for its bookkeepers. I am told you are among the finest they’ve had in some time—despite your limitations.”

“We are a fine House. It is why my grandfather is a sapiarch—”

“And yet, you are not your grandfather—nor do you possess his abilities. The council is far away, in lands fairer than these. His authority will do little for you here. Do not presume to believe otherwise. The Bretons will offer you little help—the urchin Dunmer of their court will offer even less. The reputation of your House means little here. In some quiet corners of the city, it may even bring you harm.”

Levoline took a breath. “I presume little, ser. Only that one who wishes to serve will be given the chance to do so. I believe this is mine—and I will use it for our glory, if only I am allowed to do so. I care not for the danger of the assignment—only that I am offered it in faith.” She resisted the urge to place her hand on Thorn’s hilt again.

Uuelmo leaned across the desk, his face squinting as he scrutinized the depth of her conviction. She kept her gaze locked to his, her chin only slightly lower than his own—which was nearly tall enough now to gather snow. Suddenly, as though satisfied with his appraisal, Uuelmo withdrew. Levoline held back her sigh of relief, her face maintaining its stern bearing.

“Then you will have our faith, Lady Levoline. Until your mission is completed, or you meet your end.”

”By their blades or our own,” Levoline answered with the words she’d been taught as a girl. Though she said them with conviction, something about the way they left her lips felt weak. She made no effort to shrink from them, no matter how desperate she felt to do so. At that at least, Uuelmo seemed pleased.

“Indeed. And I will remind you that it is ideal that you die by the hand of our enemies—grandfather or no, the sapiarchs will not be kind in rendering their judgement, nor will our spymaster. But I digress, clearly you have considered these things if you are already so eager to begin… Very well. A coded parcel arrived this morning with the specifics of your orders, which I have taken the liberty of deciphering. A letter was sent from the Emperor of Tamriel—Uriel Septim VII himself—to Queen Mynisera of Daggerfall. We have reason to suspect she never received it. This letter is… of a scandalous nature,” Uuelmo said the words with a smile, as though they’d been delicious to say. “Retrieving it is of the utmost importance to the council—and so it shall be to you.”

In truth, she had expected more. Word of a letter—someplace in the Province of High Rock, which meant it might as well be in Sentinel, or the Wrothgarian Mountains—was too little to follow. The Iliac Bay was a vast place. What sort of scandal could be so ruinous that the council was willing to overturn the whole of it for a single letter? Regardless, this was the chance she’d long waited for. With or without a lead, she would prove herself worthy of her line. Still, it was better that she had one. “What connections do you have among the court? What names can you give me before you depart? Surely there is something?” Though twenty years was no time at all for herself or Uuelmo, the mannish courtiers of Wayrest would no doubt have made their connections and rivalries to her retainer in haste. It was an idiosyncrasy of the lesser races she’d learnt long ago. So eager to live, and in such a hurry to die. It was no surprise when Eadwyre offered her the deed she laid claim to. Uuelmo had forged the invitation to his court, of course, and so when she first appeared before the king, Eadwyre could only pretend that he knew who she was. It was beyond the pride of such men to admit fault, she knew—and so she had found herself here, in what was soon to be her office, as a Lady of Wayrest. The lands to her name had been drawn out of the maps long ago, divided by a petty skirmish between Direnni lords and recorded in scrolls so ancient none would know where to reference them.

All save a Librarian of House Morimacil.

”Ties are taut in the house of His Majesty…” Uuelmo answered her. “His wife, Barenziah, though she loves him, seeks to place her own son—Eadwyre’s stepson—on the throne. Helseth is the oldest of the three, but the people of Wayrest will not take kindly to a Dunmer ruling over them. Most support Elysana… though she is the youngest of the three; child to Eadwyre and his late wife.”

”What is our stance in the matter?”

”Our stance?” Uuelmo’s smile curled in mock surprise, before his already squished face squinted further into a knowing look. “We are neutral in this matter—in support of the one most well suited to maintaining Wayrest as a powerful and prosperous nation.”

Helseth, then. Levoline allowed herself to mirror his smile, though she felt he held her gaze a little longer than was comfortable. “I understand,” she said, letting her eyes drift down to the desk, where she pretended to be lost in contemplation. “This will do, Uuelmo. Thank you.”

He gave her one last appraising look. “I will offer you one final chance to decline this assignment, Lady Levoline. There will be no shame if you return now. I am certain your House has no shortage of… cousins to take your place.”

She knew what he said was not entirely true, though it had not been entirely wrong either. There would be no shame in returning—not for any other Altmer. It would not be so for her. “What you say is true. And I accept this assignment in full knowledge of the consequences for failure, ser. I will not fail you, nor the spymaster.”

“If you are discovered, we will never have spoken.”

“Let it be so.” Her hand gripped Thorn’s hilt till her knuckles grew white.

Uuelmo nodded solemnly. “I see your mind is set. Good. You will need resolve to accomplish the task set before you. Light of Auri-El be with you, Lady Levoline.

Uuelmo slid a book across the desk to her.

“Welcome, at last…

…to the Thalmor.”
