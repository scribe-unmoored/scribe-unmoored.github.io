Chapter VI: Thorn

Neither wore armor, except what little protection sackcloth could offer. Neither carried a sword. One of the two men held a club in one hand, the other had a knife at his belt. He held Princess Elysana, whom had been blindfolded, gagged, and—judging by her smell and the state of her once-white dress—taken against her will through the sewers of Wayrest. Her handlers only offered Levoline a nod as she approached.

”Hurry it up, elf. Only a matter of time before we’re discovered here. Palace is probably crawling with guards by now.” One of them spoke as she entered. He had a gruff, ugly voice, and a face obscured by shadow. “Got your information—the Underking promised us safe passage out of the city once you had it—so take it already.”

Levoline understood at once what had happened. Playing with the lives of our royals, are we, Uuelmo? She was no stranger to the Underking, nor the mythology that clouded him. A great battlemage. A necromancer. An incarnation of Talos. The rumors of what he was—and if he even existed at all—were as numerous as the brothels of Camlorn. Her briefings offered clarity the locals were unlikely to accept: the Underking was a criminal, simply put. A well-connected, ruthless, and potentially useful criminal overlord, yes—but a criminal nonetheless. Knowing Uuelmo made a deal with such a man, no matter how well he might have covered his tracks—or her own—made her uneasy.

”The information then, if you please.” Levoline answered, her voice level. Whatever deal Uuelmo struck up between himself and the Underking, it was not made with her in mind. Should she take the information and be on her way? Allow the princess to be taken to whatever horrible fate might await her? No. There was more to leverage in this moment—Uuelmo must know that any fallout from this situation would be directed onto the criminal underworld of Wayrest. Better yet, there would no doubt be a reward for Elysana's savior… and where better a place for an upright Altmer to be, than saying her prayers to the Eight? Levoline stifled a smile to herself, maintaining her composure as the man approached. He leaned in close.

”The Underking knows what you are—and that your enemy has arrived in Daggerfall. They seek the same prize, and are closer to it than you realize. That’s all I was told to say. Now, where’s the passage—we would’ve left without you, if only we knew which damn passage to take.

Levoline made an effort to survey the room—as though the correct passage might reveal itself. In truth, she had no inkling of which dark threshold might keep the key to their salvation—and the Princess’ dark fate; nor did she have any intention of delivering them. She only busied herself looking to hide her surprise at his words. The Blades.

It was a heartbeat before could steady herself. Her hand went to Thorn’s hilt. There was no nervous instinct this time. Levoline was the stillest she’d been since entering the Iliac Bay. “I’m afraid you’ve made a fatal mistake, ser. Whoever your original deal was with, it would seem they’d gone craven on you. I will offer you two choices: release the Princess, in the name of the King, and be spared.”

One of the men groaned, retrieving his mace from his belt. “Self-righteous elven bitch…” The one closest to her drew his knife. “And the other?” he snarled, a cruel satisfaction creeping through his words.

Levoline answered him first by drawing Thorn, then by saying: “We settle this like gentlemen—or whatever you have here that’s closest.” The rapier’s black blade gleamed in the low candlelight. Ebony, forged from the veins of Red Mountain, inlaid with golden filigree woven over an ornate basket hilt. A gift from her father many years ago. Her constant companion in the years since.

The men took notice of the blade immediately, shrinking back before they must have realized it would turn a handsome profit. Levoline noticed the shift. The way her opponents immediately turned their attention from the Princess—to a new prize.

She caught the first’s knife as he thrust it at her, catching it in the cage of steel that protected her hand. With a twist and a jerk, she wrestled it free of his grasp. With a slash to the bicep, his arm hung uselessly at his side. The man yowled furiously. His companion pushed him aside.

He shouted something obscene, raising his mace high—

Levoline would not have been strong enough to deflect it. Two lifetimes of training had taught her limits. It taught her alternatives.

Before the blow could fall, she swiped at his hand.

A heartbeat later, and his smallest finger fell to the floor, his mace with it. He fell to his knees, clutching his leaking hand.
