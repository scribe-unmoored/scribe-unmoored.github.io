Chapter III: Privateer's Hold

4th of Morning Star, 3E405

Four hours past midnight

Somewhere in the Iliac Bay...

He wasn’t sure how long he’d been out. Only that he’d used the last of his magicka to seal the wound at his brow. The Blade was no healer. Fire had been his only recourse. A deft spell to the broken skin, taking care to avoid his eye.

He still clutched his broken sword. The fire was nearly gone now, but even with only a few embers left, there was light enough to see his immediate surroundings. He held the ruined blade to his face—two red eyes peeking back at him in his reflection. His skin, ashen grey, made him almost invisible in the gloom; his hair, raven black, hung over the cauterized wound. He was a Dunmer. For him, fire did not burn so hot as it did others. A great boon for a battlemage, he’d always been told. He fingered the burnt skin, wondering if his eyebrow would ever grow back. His skin was paler than usual. His eyes sunken.

The guarskin wrappings had begun to fall away from the hilt of his sword. The wood beneath had been carved long ago. He replaced the grip with care, sheathed it, and tried to stand. Some of his strength had returned. Even so, getting to his feet was no simple task. He steadied himself against the driftwood he’d slept against, body urging him to lay back down and die. The world spun as he got to his feet, steadying only after he had spent a long time willing it to stop.

The first shuffling steps through the cold air were agony, but then the Blade was making his way through the cave, toward the door he had seen before the landslide knocked him unconscious. There came a draft of warm, mildewed air from somewhere up ahead. He followed the scent, and the shrinking echoes of his footsteps, until he found the far wall of the cave. His fingers reached out, brushing against sea-worn wood. Then he was stepping through the threshold, and into the warmth beyond.

The room on the other side stank of mold and old wine. He kicked something that sounded like glass as he wandered into the darkness. There came other sounds too. More glass, now and then forcing him to stop and pick shards from his feet. The soft crunch of straw. Something that sounded too close to the cracking of dry bone. He could not see if the wall here were of natural stone or the sort hewn by the hands of men. He groped his way through the dark, pushing aside debris with his bare feet. At last, he came to another doorway, this one leading higher, up a staircase that led to the promising scent of cinders. He put his weight on the edge of the first stair, willing the old wood to stay silent. It creaked anyway, as though mocking him. All of the stairs did, as he ascended slowly, painfully into the ruins above.

Torch sconces lined the walls there every few spans, though the light they could cast was faint. The ceiling, dripping with water, sagged beneath the mass of earth above. The walls and floor were indeed hewn from old stone—the same flesh-colored limestone of the cave. He followed the torches, leaning against the wall. It curved gently, leading him deeper inside the hold.

Where have I found myself? he wondered. Who keeps torches in a place like this? When he realized he’d fallen asleep before, he’d half-expected to wake to the searing light of Aetherius. When instead he was met with darkness, he was sure he’d been cast into the Waters of Oblivion. But this was no infernal plane. He could hear rats, shuttling about in the shadows, their squeaks accompanied by footfalls far too large for comfort. He wondered if they could sense how weak he’d become. It would be one thing to fail in his mission—it would be another to be eaten alive by such creatures. He pushed the thought from his mind, hand ready over the hilt of his sword.

The stream of his magicka was utterly dry now—and he carried only his ruined blade. Rats did not keep these halls. Something more formidable lay deeper. He could almost sense it. He wondered if he would have the strength to face it. He wondered if there was any point to pushing forward. There was no guarantee his entrance had not been the only one.

Suddenly, there was a shriek that echoed through the tunnel like the scream of a horn-blower. The Blade saw nothing—the hall turned hard to the left ahead. Whatever was around the corner must have sensed him. He could hear it coming closer; the sound of leather wings beating frantically. There was a shadow that for a heartbeat blocked one of the torches—and then it was surging toward him, squeaking maniacally.

It was a hallmark of his order to cut as one drew. The ruined sword sang a shrill song as he freed it. The sound of the edge scraping bone was its coda. A heartbeat later, and the Blade’s ears pricked as something wet landed behind him. Another heartbeat later, and the other half of the creature landed beside it.

Pain forced its way through his body like a bolt of lightning, grounding him as though he’d been struck. The Blade dropped his sword, clutching his chest in the place where the ribs were loose.

A senseless cry tore itself from his throat, echoing through the hall. He lay there seething until he could silence himself and stand. His vision was blurred, his head pounding against the charred flesh of his brow. A dozen searing wounds reminded him of their presence. He forced them to melt away—using every last drop of willpower remaining to quiet them, one by one.

“Gods, I could have been a healer.” He said to the darkness as he was struggling to his feet. “I’m going to die in this place.” He could not say whether or not he believed the words—only that he took up his sword and pushed forward. If he was to die here, he would do so fighting his way out.

After some time, he came to a doorway, hewn into the wall and fixed with another worn, wooden door. He pushed it open, willing that at least one thing in this place would not betray him. The door came open with a malicious creak. The room on the other side looked how he imagined the one from before must have looked. It was darker than the hallway had been here, though there was light from a tripod brazier, its brass surface long patinaed. Broken bottles lay smashed across the floor. Battered crates were piled high against the walls, some stacks reaching to the ceiling. On one wooden facade, the compass of the East Empire Company was emblazoned. On another, the shield-eagle of the Summerset Isles. There were more: a trio of purple-orange dahlias for Wayrest, the red dragon of Daggerfall, the sullen-faced crescent moon of Sentinel. The only other sign the Blade could make out in the gloom was a burnished ‘R’ for the Restless League, a once-great band of rovers and pirates. A smuggler’s den this must be, and an old one at that, he realized. Somehow, the Blade thought there must be a thousand such places in the Iliac Bay, where the Emperor’s peace was a patchwork affair. He wondered how many of such dens the king of Daggerfall knew of. It wouldn’t make High Rock or Hammerfell special among the provinces if the answer was not none.

He made a brief effort to search the crates. Most were filled with trade goods—silks that would have made fine bandages if he’d known about them earlier, jars of scented candlewax, spoiled whale oil—a discovery he lamented finding immediately after—alongside no shortage of opened crates, half-full of brandy, rum, and sundry liquors. Definitely a smuggler’s den.

From the shadows between two stacks of crates, a rattle pierced the darkness. The Blade turned to it, sword in hand.

His wounds made themselves known again, though this time he was ready for them. Limping, he tried to summon a fire to his hand. The light came only for a moment before it died.

The rattling grew with the flames. Just at the edge of its light, the Blade could see two beady red eyes, gleaming balefully. There was nothing behind them, save an expression of dull, withdrawn resentment that vanished with the light. Creeping closer, the Blade could make out a body coated with blue-green skin, two thin, membranous wings stretching from the back of an emaciated, humanoid body. An imp. It screeched at his approach, battering itself bloody against the confines of its cage as he drew close. It reached for him through the bars of its cage, miniature hand flexing claws half their length. The Blade stepped back just in time to avoid them.

“Easy now,” he said, attempting to calm the creature. It grew bolder instead, thrashing, reaching through the bars of its cage until the rust raked across the creature’s bare chest. It was a pitiful sight. A poor creature, probably once intended for some alchemist’s storehouse or a king’s menagerie—never arriving, voyage beset by pirates.

“You’ve been here a good long time, haven’t you?” The Blade brought his sword to bear. The creature’s skin was wrapped tight over the bone, like a garrote of flesh. This was no way to die, starving to death in a moldy cage. He’d make it quick.

He could barely raise his arm before pain shot through his chest again. No. I’m too weak. He’d seen what an imp was capable of—travelers with their throats torn out. Horses, their flanks blown open by impish fire. One battle with such a creature would have been enough to tell him he was in no shape to face one now—and he’d seen dozens in his years. With no fire, no strength, and no sword long enough to deliver the killing blow, he could not chance dying to this creature. Not with his mission at stake.

“I’m sorry,” he said. The imp chattered madly, before answering with a blast of fire, hurled from between the bars of its cage. The blast went wide, coming close enough to his shoulder that he could feel its heat for a fraction of a second. It struck the far wall, dissipating, taking with it the brief surge of light it had brought. The Blade stumbled back from the cage, grimaced, and continued on his way.
